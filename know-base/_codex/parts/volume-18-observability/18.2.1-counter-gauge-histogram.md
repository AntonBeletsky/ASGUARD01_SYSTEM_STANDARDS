#### 18.2.1 Counter, Gauge, Histogram

##### Purpose

`Counter, Gauge, Histogram` belongs to the `Metrics` chapter of `Observability`. In this context, it is about recording enough runtime evidence to explain behavior and diagnose failures. The practical goal is to help engineers make a decision that can survive code review, design review, production operation, and later change.

This topic matters because `Observability` is concerned with making system behavior explainable from the outside during normal work and incidents. A good treatment of `Counter, Gauge, Histogram` should clarify signals, correlation, debuggability, and cost, protect the invariant that signals should be correlated, actionable, and affordable enough to keep in production, and give the team observable signals such as actionable traces, useful logs, and low-noise alerts.

##### Core Ideas

- Start with intent. State why `Counter, Gauge, Histogram` is relevant to the current problem before naming a mechanism.
- Use the chapter boundary. `Metrics` supplies the nearby vocabulary, constraints, and failure modes.
- Protect the invariant: signals should be correlated, actionable, and affordable enough to keep in production.
- Use this topic when operators need to answer what happened, where, why, and who was affected.
- Connect the decision to the review artifact: the relevant artifact is a observability review.
- Prefer evidence over preference. The best answer is the one whose behavior can be inspected, tested, measured, or safely changed.

##### Practical Guidance

- Write a one-sentence problem statement for `Counter, Gauge, Histogram` before changing code, architecture, or process.
- Identify the owner of the decision and the people who will maintain, operate, or integrate with it.
- Name the boundary: inputs, outputs, collaborators, data, trust level, lifecycle, and failure behavior.
- Choose the smallest mechanism that preserves the invariant while keeping the design understandable.
- Add an explicit review signal: test, metric, trace, decision record, schema check, benchmark, or runbook step.
- Document the trade-off in the place future maintainers will actually see during review or operation.
- Revisit the decision when scale, ownership, product behavior, compliance pressure, or failure evidence changes.

##### Trade-offs

- Specific guidance for `Counter, Gauge, Histogram` improves consistency, but applied mechanically it can ignore local constraints.
- Extra abstraction can isolate variation, but it also adds names, lifecycle, and debugging paths that must be owned.
- More validation and documentation reduce ambiguity, but they should target real risk rather than slowing harmless change.
- A local improvement can increase cross-team coordination cost if the boundary or ownership model is unclear.
- The right level of rigor depends on impact: high-risk work deserves stronger evidence than a small internal cleanup.

##### Examples

Design the diagnostic path before the incident:

```text
Question: what must an operator answer quickly.
Signal: metric, log, span, event, or profile that answers it.
Correlation: request id, trace id, user id, tenant, or deployment version.
Cardinality: what labels are safe at production scale.
Action: the decision the signal enables.
```

For `Counter, Gauge, Histogram`, observability is successful only when it shortens the path from symptom to explanation.

##### Common Mistakes

- logging volume without diagnostic value
- alerting on symptoms no one can act on
- missing correlation identifiers
- Optimizing the first implementation while ignoring migration, rollback, observability, and long-term ownership.
- Reviewing surface shape instead of the invariant, failure behavior, and change cost the section is meant to control.

#### 16.4.2 Health Checks and Load Balancing

##### Purpose

`Health Checks and Load Balancing` belongs to the `Service Coordination` chapter of `Distributed Systems`. In this context, it is about controlling time and resource use under a known workload. The practical goal is to help engineers make a decision that can survive code review, design review, production operation, and later change.

This topic matters because `Distributed Systems` is concerned with preserving behavior when clocks, networks, dependencies, and nodes fail independently. A good treatment of `Health Checks and Load Balancing` should clarify idempotency, timeouts, coordination, and degradation, protect the invariant that performance claims must be tied to workload, percentile, capacity, and measurement method, and give the team observable signals such as defined failure modes, bounded retries, and observable recovery.

##### Core Ideas

- Start with intent. State why `Health Checks and Load Balancing` is relevant to the current problem before naming a mechanism.
- Use the chapter boundary. `Service Coordination` supplies the nearby vocabulary, constraints, and failure modes.
- Protect the invariant: performance claims must be tied to workload, percentile, capacity, and measurement method.
- Use this topic when user experience, cost, or reliability depends on predictable response under load.
- Connect the decision to the review artifact: the relevant artifact is a failure-mode review.
- Prefer evidence over preference. The best answer is the one whose behavior can be inspected, tested, measured, or safely changed.

##### Practical Guidance

- Write a one-sentence problem statement for `Health Checks and Load Balancing` before changing code, architecture, or process.
- Identify the owner of the decision and the people who will maintain, operate, or integrate with it.
- Name the boundary: inputs, outputs, collaborators, data, trust level, lifecycle, and failure behavior.
- Choose the smallest mechanism that preserves the invariant while keeping the design understandable.
- Add an explicit review signal: test, metric, trace, decision record, schema check, benchmark, or runbook step.
- Document the trade-off in the place future maintainers will actually see during review or operation.
- Revisit the decision when scale, ownership, product behavior, compliance pressure, or failure evidence changes.

##### Trade-offs

- Specific guidance for `Health Checks and Load Balancing` improves consistency, but applied mechanically it can ignore local constraints.
- Extra abstraction can isolate variation, but it also adds names, lifecycle, and debugging paths that must be owned.
- More validation and documentation reduce ambiguity, but they should target real risk rather than slowing harmless change.
- A local improvement can increase cross-team coordination cost if the boundary or ownership model is unclear.
- The right level of rigor depends on impact: high-risk work deserves stronger evidence than a small internal cleanup.

##### Examples

A failure-mode note makes the design operational:

```text
Dependency or component: what can fail independently.
Timeout: the maximum time callers wait.
Retry rule: when retry is safe and when it amplifies load.
Fallback: degraded behavior that protects users or operators.
Signal: metric, trace, log, or alert that proves the path occurred.
```

For `Health Checks and Load Balancing`, the value is that the system has a defined behavior when the happy path disappears.

##### Common Mistakes

- optimizing without a baseline
- confusing average latency with tail latency
- using cache without an invalidation story
- Optimizing the first implementation while ignoring migration, rollback, observability, and long-term ownership.
- Reviewing surface shape instead of the invariant, failure behavior, and change cost the section is meant to control.

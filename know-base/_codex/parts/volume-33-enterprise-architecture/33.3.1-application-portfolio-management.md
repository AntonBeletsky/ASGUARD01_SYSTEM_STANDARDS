#### 33.3.1 Application Portfolio Management

##### Purpose

`Application Portfolio Management` belongs to the `Application Landscape` chapter of `Enterprise Architecture`. In this context, it is about making `Application Portfolio Management` explicit as a decision area within Application Landscape. The practical goal is to help engineers make a decision that can survive code review, design review, production operation, and later change.

This topic matters because `Enterprise Architecture` is concerned with aligning systems, capabilities, data, and governance across a large organization. A good treatment of `Application Portfolio Management` should clarify capabilities, integration, portfolio, and standards, protect the invariant that the team can explain how `Application Portfolio Management` changes behavior, ownership, or risk, and give the team observable signals such as coherent roadmaps, reused capabilities, and fewer integration surprises.

##### Core Ideas

- Start with intent. State why `Application Portfolio Management` is relevant to the current problem before naming a mechanism.
- Use the chapter boundary. `Application Landscape` supplies the nearby vocabulary, constraints, and failure modes.
- Protect the invariant: the team can explain how `Application Portfolio Management` changes behavior, ownership, or risk.
- Use this topic when work in Enterprise Architecture depends on capabilities, integration, portfolio, and standards.
- Connect the decision to the review artifact: the relevant artifact is a enterprise architecture review.
- Prefer evidence over preference. The best answer is the one whose behavior can be inspected, tested, measured, or safely changed.

##### Practical Guidance

- Write a one-sentence problem statement for `Application Portfolio Management` before changing code, architecture, or process.
- Identify the owner of the decision and the people who will maintain, operate, or integrate with it.
- Name the boundary: inputs, outputs, collaborators, data, trust level, lifecycle, and failure behavior.
- Choose the smallest mechanism that preserves the invariant while keeping the design understandable.
- Add an explicit review signal: test, metric, trace, decision record, schema check, benchmark, or runbook step.
- Document the trade-off in the place future maintainers will actually see during review or operation.
- Revisit the decision when scale, ownership, product behavior, compliance pressure, or failure evidence changes.

##### Trade-offs

- Specific guidance for `Application Portfolio Management` improves consistency, but applied mechanically it can ignore local constraints.
- Extra abstraction can isolate variation, but it also adds names, lifecycle, and debugging paths that must be owned.
- More validation and documentation reduce ambiguity, but they should target real risk rather than slowing harmless change.
- A local improvement can increase cross-team coordination cost if the boundary or ownership model is unclear.
- The right level of rigor depends on impact: high-risk work deserves stronger evidence than a small internal cleanup.

##### Common Mistakes

- treating `Application Portfolio Management` as terminology rather than an operational decision
- copying a familiar pattern without checking constraints
- leaving success and failure criteria implicit
- Optimizing the first implementation while ignoring migration, rollback, observability, and long-term ownership.
- Reviewing surface shape instead of the invariant, failure behavior, and change cost the section is meant to control.

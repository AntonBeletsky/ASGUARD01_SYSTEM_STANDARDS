#### 34.3.2 Access Control Reviews

##### Purpose

`Access Control Reviews` belongs to the `Audit and Controls` chapter of `Compliance and Risk`. In this context, it is about binding actions to trustworthy identity and explicit permissions. The practical goal is to help engineers make a decision that can survive code review, design review, production operation, and later change.

This topic matters because `Compliance and Risk` is concerned with turning regulatory and operational risk into explicit, testable engineering obligations. A good treatment of `Access Control Reviews` should clarify controls, evidence, auditability, and risk ownership, protect the invariant that every sensitive action must be authorized against the current actor, resource, and context, and give the team observable signals such as available evidence, controlled exceptions, and reduced audit surprises.

##### Core Ideas

- Start with intent. State why `Access Control Reviews` is relevant to the current problem before naming a mechanism.
- Use the chapter boundary. `Audit and Controls` supplies the nearby vocabulary, constraints, and failure modes.
- Protect the invariant: every sensitive action must be authorized against the current actor, resource, and context.
- Use this topic when a system must decide who is acting and what they are allowed to do.
- Connect the decision to the review artifact: the relevant artifact is a risk and compliance review.
- Prefer evidence over preference. The best answer is the one whose behavior can be inspected, tested, measured, or safely changed.

##### Practical Guidance

- Write a one-sentence problem statement for `Access Control Reviews` before changing code, architecture, or process.
- Identify the owner of the decision and the people who will maintain, operate, or integrate with it.
- Name the boundary: inputs, outputs, collaborators, data, trust level, lifecycle, and failure behavior.
- Choose the smallest mechanism that preserves the invariant while keeping the design understandable.
- Add an explicit review signal: test, metric, trace, decision record, schema check, benchmark, or runbook step.
- Document the trade-off in the place future maintainers will actually see during review or operation.
- Revisit the decision when scale, ownership, product behavior, compliance pressure, or failure evidence changes.

##### Trade-offs

- Specific guidance for `Access Control Reviews` improves consistency, but applied mechanically it can ignore local constraints.
- Extra abstraction can isolate variation, but it also adds names, lifecycle, and debugging paths that must be owned.
- More validation and documentation reduce ambiguity, but they should target real risk rather than slowing harmless change.
- A local improvement can increase cross-team coordination cost if the boundary or ownership model is unclear.
- The right level of rigor depends on impact: high-risk work deserves stronger evidence than a small internal cleanup.

##### Examples

Connect the control to an abuse case and evidence:

```text
Asset: the data, action, or identity being protected.
Threat: the realistic way it can be misused.
Control: the preventive or detective mechanism.
Evidence: log, test, audit event, or review artifact proving the control exists.
Failure response: what happens when the control fails or is bypassed.
```

For `Access Control Reviews`, this keeps the section grounded in risk reduction instead of security theater.

##### Common Mistakes

- checking only UI visibility
- trusting stale roles
- mixing authentication with authorization decisions
- Optimizing the first implementation while ignoring migration, rollback, observability, and long-term ownership.
- Reviewing surface shape instead of the invariant, failure behavior, and change cost the section is meant to control.

##### Review Checklist

- [ ] The reason to apply `Access Control Reviews` is clear in the current `Audit and Controls` context.
- [ ] The invariant is stated: every sensitive action must be authorized against the current actor, resource, and context.
- [ ] The design names owners, boundaries, collaborators, and failure behavior.
- [ ] Evidence exists through available evidence, controlled exceptions, and reduced audit surprises.
- [ ] The trade-off is explicit enough that a future maintainer can revisit it.
- [ ] The solution is no more complex than the risk or variation requires.

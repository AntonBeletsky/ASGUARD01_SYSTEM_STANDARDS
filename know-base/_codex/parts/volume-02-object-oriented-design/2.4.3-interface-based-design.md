#### 2.4.3 Interface-Based Design

##### Purpose

`Interface-Based Design` belongs to the `Composition vs Inheritance` chapter of `Object Oriented Design`. In this context, it is about making `Interface-Based Design` explicit as a decision area within Composition vs Inheritance. The practical goal is to help engineers make a decision that can survive code review, design review, production operation, and later change.

This topic matters because `Object Oriented Design` is concerned with assigning responsibilities so objects collaborate through stable, meaningful boundaries. A good treatment of `Interface-Based Design` should clarify responsibility, encapsulation, coupling, and substitution, protect the invariant that the team can explain how `Interface-Based Design` changes behavior, ownership, or risk, and give the team observable signals such as small public APIs, testable collaborations, and limited ripple effects.

##### Core Ideas

- Start with intent. State why `Interface-Based Design` is relevant to the current problem before naming a mechanism.
- Use the chapter boundary. `Composition vs Inheritance` supplies the nearby vocabulary, constraints, and failure modes.
- Protect the invariant: the team can explain how `Interface-Based Design` changes behavior, ownership, or risk.
- Use this topic when work in Object Oriented Design depends on responsibility, encapsulation, coupling, and substitution.
- Connect the decision to the review artifact: the relevant artifact is a object model review.
- Prefer evidence over preference. The best answer is the one whose behavior can be inspected, tested, measured, or safely changed.

##### Practical Guidance

- Write a one-sentence problem statement for `Interface-Based Design` before changing code, architecture, or process.
- Identify the owner of the decision and the people who will maintain, operate, or integrate with it.
- Name the boundary: inputs, outputs, collaborators, data, trust level, lifecycle, and failure behavior.
- Choose the smallest mechanism that preserves the invariant while keeping the design understandable.
- Add an explicit review signal: test, metric, trace, decision record, schema check, benchmark, or runbook step.
- Document the trade-off in the place future maintainers will actually see during review or operation.
- Revisit the decision when scale, ownership, product behavior, compliance pressure, or failure evidence changes.

##### Trade-offs

- Specific guidance for `Interface-Based Design` improves consistency, but applied mechanically it can ignore local constraints.
- Extra abstraction can isolate variation, but it also adds names, lifecycle, and debugging paths that must be owned.
- More validation and documentation reduce ambiguity, but they should target real risk rather than slowing harmless change.
- A local improvement can increase cross-team coordination cost if the boundary or ownership model is unclear.
- The right level of rigor depends on impact: high-risk work deserves stronger evidence than a small internal cleanup.

##### Examples

A concise technical note is usually enough:

```text
Context: what problem is being solved.
Constraint: what must remain true.
Decision: what changes in design, implementation, or process.
Evidence: what shows the decision is working.
Review trigger: what would prove the decision wrong.
```

For `Interface-Based Design`, this structure keeps the section tied to behavior and consequences rather than abstract advice.

##### Common Mistakes

- treating `Interface-Based Design` as terminology rather than an operational decision
- copying a familiar pattern without checking constraints
- leaving success and failure criteria implicit
- Optimizing the first implementation while ignoring migration, rollback, observability, and long-term ownership.
- Reviewing surface shape instead of the invariant, failure behavior, and change cost the section is meant to control.

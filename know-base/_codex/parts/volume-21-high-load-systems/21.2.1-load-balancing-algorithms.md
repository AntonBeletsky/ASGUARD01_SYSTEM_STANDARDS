#### 21.2.1 Load Balancing Algorithms

##### Purpose

`Load Balancing Algorithms` belongs to the `Traffic Management` chapter of `High Load Systems`. In this context, it is about controlling time and resource use under a known workload. The practical goal is to help engineers make a decision that can survive code review, design review, production operation, and later change.

This topic matters because `High Load Systems` is concerned with keeping systems predictable when demand, concurrency, and data volume exceed ordinary assumptions. A good treatment of `Load Balancing Algorithms` should clarify backpressure, hotspots, queues, and capacity, protect the invariant that performance claims must be tied to workload, percentile, capacity, and measurement method, and give the team observable signals such as controlled saturation, known bottlenecks, and graceful degradation.

##### Trade-offs

- Specific guidance for `Load Balancing Algorithms` improves consistency, but applied mechanically it can ignore local constraints.
- Extra abstraction can isolate variation, but it also adds names, lifecycle, and debugging paths that must be owned.
- More validation and documentation reduce ambiguity, but they should target real risk rather than slowing harmless change.
- A local improvement can increase cross-team coordination cost if the boundary or ownership model is unclear.
- The right level of rigor depends on impact: high-risk work deserves stronger evidence than a small internal cleanup.

##### Common Mistakes

- optimizing without a baseline
- confusing average latency with tail latency
- using cache without an invalidation story
- Optimizing the first implementation while ignoring migration, rollback, observability, and long-term ownership.
- Reviewing surface shape instead of the invariant, failure behavior, and change cost the section is meant to control.

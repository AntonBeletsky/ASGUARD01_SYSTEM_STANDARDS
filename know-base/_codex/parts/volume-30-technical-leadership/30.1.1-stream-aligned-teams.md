#### 30.1.1 Stream-Aligned Teams

##### Purpose

`Stream-Aligned Teams` belongs to the `Team Topologies` chapter of `Technical Leadership`. In this context, it is about communicating state changes or work through asynchronous records. The practical goal is to help engineers make a decision that can survive code review, design review, production operation, and later change.

This topic matters because `Technical Leadership` is concerned with turning technical ambiguity into aligned decisions, execution, and learning. A good treatment of `Stream-Aligned Teams` should clarify alignment, mentorship, decision quality, and communication, protect the invariant that consumers must handle ordering, duplication, retries, and schema evolution explicitly, and give the team observable signals such as clear direction, better reviews, and less rework.

##### Core Ideas

- Start with intent. State why `Stream-Aligned Teams` is relevant to the current problem before naming a mechanism.
- Use the chapter boundary. `Team Topologies` supplies the nearby vocabulary, constraints, and failure modes.
- Protect the invariant: consumers must handle ordering, duplication, retries, and schema evolution explicitly.
- Use this topic when producers and consumers should be decoupled in time, scale, or ownership.
- Connect the decision to the review artifact: the relevant artifact is a technical leadership review.
- Prefer evidence over preference. The best answer is the one whose behavior can be inspected, tested, measured, or safely changed.

##### Practical Guidance

- Write a one-sentence problem statement for `Stream-Aligned Teams` before changing code, architecture, or process.
- Identify the owner of the decision and the people who will maintain, operate, or integrate with it.
- Name the boundary: inputs, outputs, collaborators, data, trust level, lifecycle, and failure behavior.
- Choose the smallest mechanism that preserves the invariant while keeping the design understandable.
- Add an explicit review signal: test, metric, trace, decision record, schema check, benchmark, or runbook step.
- Document the trade-off in the place future maintainers will actually see during review or operation.
- Revisit the decision when scale, ownership, product behavior, compliance pressure, or failure evidence changes.

##### Trade-offs

- Specific guidance for `Stream-Aligned Teams` improves consistency, but applied mechanically it can ignore local constraints.
- Extra abstraction can isolate variation, but it also adds names, lifecycle, and debugging paths that must be owned.
- More validation and documentation reduce ambiguity, but they should target real risk rather than slowing harmless change.
- A local improvement can increase cross-team coordination cost if the boundary or ownership model is unclear.
- The right level of rigor depends on impact: high-risk work deserves stronger evidence than a small internal cleanup.

##### Common Mistakes

- assuming exactly-once delivery
- publishing vague events
- forgetting replay and backfill behavior
- Optimizing the first implementation while ignoring migration, rollback, observability, and long-term ownership.
- Reviewing surface shape instead of the invariant, failure behavior, and change cost the section is meant to control.
